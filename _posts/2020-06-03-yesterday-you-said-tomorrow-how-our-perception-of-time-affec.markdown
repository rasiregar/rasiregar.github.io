## Warped sense of reality

This book I am reading, [The Willpower Instinct by Kelly McGonigal](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/10865206-the-willpower-instinct?ac=1&from_search=true&qid=TtEBN5OA5E&rank=1), is a revelation, to say the least. In this book, McGonigal gathered numerous studies about the scientific basis of willpower, from the psychological to even neurological point of view and arranged it into one, single coherent writing to describe how our willpower works.

From this book also she reported the findings that what makes us fall into distractions, procrastination, and impulsive actions: warped perception of time.

It turns out what makes us do irrational things above is perpetuated in these two ways we wrongly perceive time:**1) thinking we will live forever, or 2) thinking we will die tomorrow.**The first concept leads us to put off things that we don’t like doing, the second leads us to do things we like first.

The first concept is more scientifically termed as**delayed discounting**. This is proven by a famous study conducted by [Rosati et al. in 2007](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17900899/), comparing chimps and actual humans in delaying gratification. Chimps subjects were given two grapes and said that if they wait two minutes, they will be given four additional grapes. In human subjects, they are given two M&Ms. The results are staggering: most of the chimps actually had the capacity to delay the gratification in order to get a higher quantity of food. Meanwhile, less than 20% of the human subjects waited for two minutes. If we see it roughly, we are beaten by chimps in delaying gratification.

Of course, this result cannot be interpreted in face value, it doesn’t do the study justice. Arguably human is the only organism that has the capability to think prospectively about the future, as Yuval Noah Harari put it in [Sapiens: A brief history of humankind.](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/23692271-sapiens?from_search=true&from_srp=true&qid=xQ24q9gfJ5&rank=1)That what makes humans a superpower force on this planet. But, the physiology of prospective thinking, while a very effective method to ensure survival in the food gathering phase in the prehistoric age, not really helpful in the times of advanced society with relatively abundant sources of primary needs.

The latter concept about thinking we will die tomorrow is associated with what it is called**terror management theory**. This theory in general says that humans essentially cannot escape the most primal fear of all: the fear of death. This is why when we are reminded of our mortality (that is definite), we will cling to something to comfort us, whether it is food and entertainment to distract or religion and philosophy to give meaning to our death. This tendency may be the cause why, as Charles Duhigg wrote in[The Power of Habit](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/12609433-the-power-of-habit?ac=1&from_search=true&qid=RdQlgvoQFm&rank=1), most people with addiction problems trying to quit will fail most frequently when some major life stress happens. The major life stresses, essentially associated with the threat of mortality or insignificance of life, disrupts our way of thinking so much, causing major discomfort, that made us very prone to things that give us instant gratification. Not only in major life stresses, but our daily lives are also filled with grim news and stories that make us activate our fight-or-flight response, blocking our rational thoughts to base our action.

## The most consistent power of all

Now we have talked about how most of the time we perceive time wrongly. Then, how should we perceive time? We don’t know when we will die, obviously.

*That’s the point*. We don’t actually know how the future will ensue. It is the major mystery that we open slightly with every second we pass, changing the future into the present. However, if there is an undeniable fact about time is that**we are living right at this precise moment**. We are currently exchanging air with the environment.

René Descartes said*cogito, ergo sum*. I think, therefore I am. Therefore the debate whether we really exist ends when we are actually thinking. We exist. Hooray! We are present in this time and space. Now what we should do to make the best out of it?

To shift from the philosophical discourse to a more practical strategy of how to improve our productivity, I hereby introduce the concept of**appeal to time**. When we acknowledge that we cannot grasp the concept of the future clearly, we appeal to the absoluteness of the present. When we are feeling that we are not consistent, we are appealing to the most consistent power of all time: the time itself.

Because time is constantly running. It will not slow down, it will not get faster. It stays where it's at. Constant rate. When we are sleeping, it keeps ticking. When we are having the best days, it ticks the same. The same goes for our worst days. Tick, tick, tick, tock.

![Sundial doesn't give the tick-tock sound, sadly. Photo by dariusz piosik on Unsplash](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/800/0*7DoMY0s8uLgSWuXu)

*Sundial doesn't give the tick-tock sound, sadly. Photo by dariusz piosik on Unsplash*Therefore, I bring my timer wherever I go, whether it is my phone or my watch. There is a**10-minute rule** that Kelly said in the book that can help us not put off our responsibilities or stick to our addiction. If you are thinking about coming back to do your undesirable habit, how about putting a 10-minute timer and see if you still want to do it later after the timer rings. Research shows that it significantly decreases your impulse by making the best use of the delayed discounting effect.

What if you want to force yourself to do things that you like to put off? Easy, just reverse the rule. Put a 10-minute timer, do your thing, and after the bell rings, ask yourself if you want to stop. Most would not because of the momentum gained by starting the activity. It’s really true when some said that the secret of getting ahead is by getting started.

The 10-minute rule is effective in isolated actions when we are faced with choices to delay gratification or to take the gratification right away. Now the question is how to actually implement it in our daily lives? The answer is by making it a habit. Gary Keller said [in his book](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/16256798-the-one-thing?ac=1&from_search=true&qid=b1O1bYvscm&rank=1) that we successfully embed a habit into our lives after doing it daily for 66 consecutive days. So, take it slow. Excellence takes time. Remember we are not racing with anyone. We just want to be our best selves.

In conclusion, the great constant force of time enables us to work efficiently and to allocate our actions in a separate manner. Make the best use of it so we can increase our productivity and quality of life.

It is easier said than done, I know. But, I hope by knowing the physiological basis of how we think and process our thoughts, we are a step closer to actuating our dreams into reality.
