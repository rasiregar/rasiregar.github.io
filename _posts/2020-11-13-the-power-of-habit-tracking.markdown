> We are what we repeatedly do. Excellence, then, is not an act, but a habit.

This is my main motto of self-development. To achieve excellence, we have to do things excellently, not once, nor twice, but many times; so many that we can deliver excellent results repeatedly with ease. The word identity was originally [derived from the medieval Latin word*identitatem*](https://www.etymonline.com/word/identity#etymonline_v_1484), which means ‘sameness’. The word itself confirms that identity lies in what human are doing repeatedly.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/800/0*S_o5x4EL66CGKHGa)

We play our roles in the community. We thrive as a society by cooperating with each other effectively, trusting others to do things that we need but we can’t obtain by ourselves. Because of that, we need to excel in our roles so we could be perceived as dependable by society. By being trustworthy and dependable, we can also reach satisfaction in life by giving excellent work and doing meaningful roles.

This is a reality that I am late to understand. I was (or still am) inconsistent in delivering my work. The quality of my output over time is like spikes of excellence with plateaus of mediocrity in between. As I am getting to understand the core of excellence, I am working to develop my consistency, step by step. Albeit slow, it is still progress nonetheless. And** developing oneself is a race with our own potential**, not others, so I don’t need to rush or feeling inferior to others.


In the meantime, I want to give you a little update about my life. So, I have just completed my pediatrics clinical rotation. In my college, this rotation is known for its grueling and exhaustive schedule. Like a cherry on top, this rotation ends with a clinical exam as its finals. A clinical exam is a type of the exam where medical students do a complete history taking with its physical examinations to a real patient, supervised by the attendings, and then continued with oral examination with the examiners discussing the case.

Because of the ongoing Covid-19 pandemic, the clinical rotation is being altered so that it minimizes our contact with the patients (to minimize the chance of being infected), thus reducing our hospital hours greatly. Still, the attendings have high expectations among us, and we have to adapt and continue studying in these unprecedented times.

The 9-week rotation is finally done and I can finally catch some breaths. However, in the final weeks of the rotation, I was struggling with learning all of the materials for the exam that I spend all my attention and time to study. My already established habits (that I made to improve my quality of life, like reading a book and exercise) fell apart. For those tiresome seven and eight days, in my mind, there was only one thing — the hope to pass this exam.

I am glad I have finally passed the exam. I, however, felt a little bit worn out for the next couple of days. My intention to get back doing my habit ended as wishful thinking. I was so emotionally drained, and in my mind, I just wanted to comfort myself to compensate for the last hell of a week. So, as expected from people my age, I resorted to playing mobile games and scrolling mindlessly on Twitter. It was not a high-quality recreation, I concede, but it gave dopamine spurts that my primitive brain needed.

Such situations exist in our lives. Situations that demand all of our attention and time because so much is at stake. It can be a major presentation at a conference, or maybe pitching a project to potential sponsors, or playing in a large gig. These types of situations will suck all your attention and energy (for a good reason). But beware if you happen to be growing a habit at that time — it might blur your focus on developing it and will risk abandonment afterward.

It had happened to me before, but it never happens again.

Why? Because now I track my habits.

## What’s so special about tracking habits anyway?

Some of you might be familiar with the title of this article, adapting from the book by Charles Duhigg, [The Power of Habit](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/12609433-the-power-of-habit?ac=1&from_search=true&qid=IZgH30p9ML&rank=1). While I have briefly explained the importance of habit at the beginning of the article, now I am going to shift the focus to the importance of tracking it.

What is habit tracking? Habit tracking, with the phrase explaining itself, is to record our action (or inaction) that we intend to do (or not do). It can be done with anything that leaves a mark that we have done (or not done) something. The stand-up comedian and TV producer Jerry Seinfeld[famously crossed a date in the calendar with a marker for every day he wrote a joke](https://jamesclear.com/stop-procrastinating-seinfeld-strategy). His goal was “not to end the streak” and by doing it, he developed consistency gradually. I prefer to use a habit tracker app that you can find on the app store (there are [tons](https://www.apple.com/search/habit-trackers?src=globalnav)). Or perhaps you prefer a more traditional approach, using a small notebook and pen, whatever floats your boat.

So why tracking habits are so essential in developing one? Because it forces us to be accountable.

You see, when you are trying to grow a habit, you want to be consistent in doing (or not doing, you get it now) a particular action. At the beginning of growing a habit, we are usually packed with motivation to embody this particular activity in our routine. However, as time goes, our mood swings (as all people do) and now we are not that excited to do it. Adding to that, the more you do it, the less appealing the activity is. Before long, you are already bored.

Life is full of surprise, and that surprise can be in a form of high stake situations I mentioned earlier. With these unexpected situations took our attention, an untracked habit will be left forgotten once we have done those things.

There is a reason why we have habits. Habits are regularities. We seek regularities because it fills our essential need for security. When we are doing things we don’t regularly do, we need a considerable amount of willpower. You can read more about this from this article by Thomas Oppong.
The Neuroscience of Change: How to Train Your Brain to Create Better Habits“The chains of habit are too weak to be felt until they are too strong to be broken.” — Samuel Johnsonmedium.com
Growing a new habit can be a draining task because we are yet to be familiar with it. It evokes a sense of unfamiliarity that makes us uncomfortable. But, as we keep doing it, we will become more relaxed, and less and less willpower needed to do it. When we do it at an extended period of time, we will need almost no willpower to do this particular thing. Habit is successfully ingrained.

Excellent people will have no trouble in delivering excellent results because it is what they are usually doing. Perhaps, it is even harder for them to give bad results because it is not regular for them.

Now back to the habit tracking. By being accountable for our habit, we don’t rely our consistency of doing it to our mood (fact: they are not reliable at all!). We are recording our actions and with it, we can analyze our progress and reflect more objectively. When we have quantitative information (our habit log), it is much easier for us to analyze our success or failure in growing a habit when coupled with all the subjective life events that surround it.

## Having a relapse? No Problem!

Even after doing all the habit-forming tips from all the reliable sources, we will still regress slip into our past self sometimes. It is very humane for us to do this. In fact, it is vital to acknowledge these phases of relapse according to psychologist James Prochaska and colleagues’ famous [**Transtheoretical Model of Behavior Change**](https://psycnet.apa.org/record/2008-17602-004).

![Transtheoretical Model of Behavior Change. The diagram is cited from: https://sphweb.bumc.bu.edu/otlt/mph-modules/sb/behavioralchangetheories/behavioralchangetheories6.html](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/800/0*XUYed0mEvMHiIH8G.png)

*Transtheoretical Model of Behavior Change. The diagram is cited from: https://sphweb.bumc.bu.edu/otlt/mph-modules/sb/behavioralchangetheories/behavioralchangetheories6.html*

In this model, the cycle starts from us not intending to change the existing behavior (*pre-contemplation*). And then, we start to think about changing our behavior (*contemplation and determination*). Once we are determined to do it, we start to make real action (*action*). But, our change of behavior mostly does not happen overnight or on the first try. We will*relapse*to our old behavior at some point.**The make or break phase happens in the maintenance phase**. How we perceive these ‘failures’ and work on it will determine the continuity, hence the success of the behavior change.

This is the part where habit tracking becomes extraordinarily helpful. The habit of tracking our habit will make it easier for us to evaluate much we have regressed so we can regain control of our habit. We will also meet those ‘high stake situations’ that prone to distract us from focusing on our growing habit. By logging our habits, these dreaded occurrences will be not much of a problem. We*will *resort back to our old habits, that’s fine, but we will get back knowing where we left it.


## A Journey to Celebrate

So back to my own story after completing the pediatrics rotation. My habits fell apart, I know, but I am not too worried about that. Thanks to my habit of tracking habits, I know how much I regress and I can continue picking up momentum from where I left off.

Habits are essential in our lives because we can’t do things just with our willpower. I can’t be a good student if I depend my study time on my spikes of high motivation. Nor I can’t be a good writer I want to become if I just write when I feel like to. Almost all great things in life require great time and effort. And by being consistent in doing small chunks of effort every day, we will be closer to our goals, chunk by chunk.

I am not in any way a habit master (Tim Ferriss or James Clear would be the right person for that), but I can guarantee you that**this (habit tracking) works big time**. It is arguably the most effective way of growing a habit. If you have tracked your habits, please continue! If you want to change yourself but you haven't thought of changing your habit and tracking them, this is a good start.

The process of developing oneself is an intimate journey that is unique for each of us. How we treat ourselves, how we talk to ourselves through success and failures affect much of our mood and happiness. The small step we take by changing minuscule habits in our lives will accumulate and giving great interest in change without realizing it. By making change normal, we will make stagnancy peculiar.
