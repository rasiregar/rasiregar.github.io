## “The only true wisdom is in knowing you know nothing”

When I first read this famous quote by Greek philosopher Socrates many years ago, I didn’t get it. What do you mean by ‘wisdom is knowing that you know nothing’? It doesn't make any sense. Socrates is the teacher of the famous philosopher Plato. As a matter of fact, Plato is the teacher of the icon of Greek philosophy: Aristotle. This little fact strengthens the notion that Socrates is the ‘grandfather of Western philosophy’.

![Sculpture of Socrates](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/800/0*0IiAviU-zFTjN9ZC.jpg)

*Sculpture of Socrates*If he is truly the man with that extraordinary claim, how can he know nothing? What is then taught to Plato, Socrates, and so on? So no wonder laypeople think philosophy is just hillybilly bullshit. He doesn’t even know what he is talking about. Ha!


As life went on, I just forget to ponder about this quote and move on with my life. For much of my years, I study many things from many sources. I also read philosophy a little (but not ancient greek philosophy particularly).

Just a few days ago, I read this gold of a Twitter thread.

In short: this thread tells you about 4 types of knowledge: Doxa, Episteme, Gnosis, and Metis. Doxa means common beliefs that spread between people, like gossip (exactly like gossip). Episteme, on the other hand, means scientific knowledge, that is obtained by reasoning or experiments. Gnosis means insight that we get from reflecting our experience upon existence. Lastly, metis means practical wisdom (perhaps this term is similar to the word*techne*).

## Everybody is genius

This thread, albeit short, has changed my outlook on the world. It fills my knowledge gap. Everything started to make sense.

Now I understand why middle-aged people tend to fall on hoaxes. It is because when they are young, they rely so much on*doxa*, gossips, and common belief. Access to the internet was a lot harder back in their age. When finally accurate information can be obtained within our fingers, some would stick to old habits. You know, old habits die hard.

I began to appreciate craftsmanship more. People who excel at their job don’t get good by reading books and do what the books say. They practice a lot. With trial and error. Those mistakes, those shortcomings will be recorded in their memory, and as time goes by, they got slightly better. That is metis.

That is why experience is deemed as the best teacher. You can’t help but learn what you have experienced.

Medicine is partly craftmanship. Knowing the efficacy of one treatment over the other is*episteme. *But a lot of practical skills, like delivering a baby, inserting intravenous access,*et cetera* are metis. Sadly, I missed a lot of opportunities to hone my metis because of the Covid-19 pandemic.

Perhaps what Albert Einstein said is true. Everybody is genius.

## I know nothing

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/800/0*TzFl-qwX9PgHqbkv)

The most important lesson of this is — I know shit about anything. I thought by reading books, I would know a thing or two about the world. In fact, I don’t. The world is so complex and vast. The knowledge comes in so many forms, that a lifetime cannot grasp even just 0.1% of it.

The worst part is believing that I know a thing or two. I am deeply humbled.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/800/1*VbcUu3n8FZf4v4AFMcVW0w.jpeg)

Humbling experiences is really ego-scathing. It really is. The dissonance I felt in my mind days thinking about this is discomforting. But it is necessary. That’s how I update my knowledge about the world. The world is complex. And in this age when knowledge is vast and very much accessible, the ability to physically (the act of studying) and mentally learn (i.e. having Socratic humility to accept knowledge of all kinds) is becoming increasingly essential.

Everything around me, both the living and the lifeless, convey a pearl of wisdom for me to learn. For that, I thank the world.
