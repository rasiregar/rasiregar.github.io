In the last four years, my life unraveled in a way that I could not expect. Spending the last four new years eve living in different city, gaining new friends from all over the world, learning and delving in a completely new field, and so on. It is not an overstatement to say that these past four years, has been the most decorated part of my 26 years of life.

So I want to cherish it by writing about it. Recalling all the lesson learned, the foolish experiences, and of course, the unforgettable moment. Because I learnt so much about life from these years, arguably more than I have been in all the years before.

And who I am writing this for, anyway? This is for those who are curious about how I see the world. And also for those who were*actually *part of my journey and wanting to see how I see them. Or maybe it is for those who happen to stumble across this badly written piece of memoir.

More than that, I am writing this for myself. Because, even if nobody reads it,*I will*. It is the way I cherish and honor my ever decaying memories. When you think core memories are going to stay with us forever — think again. Alzheimer’s is a horrible disease. Whereas I do not wish myself to have it later in life, I cannot guarantee that I will be free to it. It is a life reminder that nothing, I mean*nothing*, last forever. Not even our thoughts.

One important disclaimer. Generative Artificial Intelligence (AI) has been booming these past years and certainly have assisted us in many parts of life. However, in terms of creative works, I detest it. I do not know if it is just me, but I always feel let down when seeing some pictures or reading a prose and realizing that it is made by AI. You know, the em dashes — like that or the overused style (*it’s not only X, it’s Y*) that gave it away. So I made a deal with myself, that I will not incorporate any AI assistance towards this writing, not even proofreading and grammar correction. So you might see ineffective sentences or inconsistent styles, but at least you can be 100% sure it is written by me. English is not my first language after all.

I never consider myself as an astrology enthusiast, but it feels prophetic for an aquarian to feel peace when exposed with bodies of water. There are some characteristics that makes water so soothing to observe. It is fluid, but visible unlike air. It is dynamic, but not rebellious like fire. It is grounded, but not static (like soil).

I have wondered to myself whether there is any big human settlement that does not have access to large bodies of water. London has Thames, Chicago has Lake Michigan, Tokyo has*Tōkyō-wan.*

The exceptions that I can think of are Madrid and Riyadh. But in general, water represents life. Chaotic yet tranquil, kinetic yet prophetic. It can drive great force like tsunamis, yet it can follow the shape of its vessel.

And this is about bodies of water that shaped me.


## Prologue: Capital

![The most populated city on earth: Jakarta. This city has more people than the whole of Canada.](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1200/1*56vD-uDX0r9_alsN3FFbdw.png)

*The most populated city on earth: Jakarta. This city has more people than the whole of Canada.*Since I was born, I had always lived in my family home. Until high school, my life never revolved beyond Tebet, one of the*kecamatan *(subdistricts) in South Jakarta. I completed all my schooling: preschool, elementary, junior high, and senior high within a five-kilometer radius from home. It was rather convenient, but also cloistered.

During my college years, there was a slight progress. I enrolled in medical school at Universitas Indonesia, located in Depok, a satellite city just south of Jakarta. Still, I never moved out of my family home, since the university was only a 25-minute train ride away — and the nearest station just a five-minute walk from my house. This remained my reality from birth until I finally took my oath as a medical doctor in April 2022.

Looking back, I have often pondered this. It was a convenient life, one where I knew the neighborhood inside out. But I also felt that I would not truly grow as a person if I never left the place that had formed the backdrop of the first two decades of my life. Do not get me wrong, I tried to compensate for this geographic immobility. I read a lot, watched a wide range of films, and listened to music from around the world. But deep down, I knew that growth required something else: to live independently, somewhere new and unfamiliar. That, to me, was the only way to evolve.

Then, that vision materialized almost unexpectedly. It felt like one small decision led to another. From trying out a scholarship application for Master’s study (which I miraculously receive), to choosing to serve my internship in a remote part of Indonesia, then pursuing my Master’s at one of the world’s most prestigious universities, and finally interning at the World Health Organization in Geneva, Switzerland.

This four-year journey has been nothing short of transformative. It completely reshaped my outlook on life. From Rote to London to Geneva — each phase has offered its own lessons, challenges, and revelations.

As I began to write this in Geneva, I could not help but feel that this might mark the end of the most adventurous phase of my life. To honor this period, I’ve decided to write this rather lengthy memoir. Even so, I know that no matter how many words I commit to the page, they will never fully capture the richness of these experiences. Still, I hope this offers a glimpse, fragments of memory and reflection that speak to what these past three years have meant to me.


## Chapter 1: Sea

![Rote Island.](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1200/1*HYC4vqBp4M720rvyGtYZxg.png)

*Rote Island.*

> “Dari Sabang sampai Merauke, dari Miangas sampai Pulau Rote.”(trans. From Sabang to Merauke, from Miangas to Rote.)

The above byword is commonly regurgitated by Indonesians to describe the breadth of this country, which spans from Sabang, Aceh (westernmost point) to Merauke, Papua (Easternmost point), Miangas (northenmost point) to Rote (southernmost point).

### Why Rote?

Indonesia is a country of more than 270 million people and 18 thousands islands, but more than half of those population live in one island: Java. This makes economic growth inequality inevitable. As someone who has lived all his life in Java, especially the capital Jakarta, it is easy to be unaware of how different the life is for average people in Java and average people outside Java. Hence, this is my main motivation of serving my medical internship in East Nusa Tenggara (*Nusa Tenggara Timur/*NTT) after graduating medical school.

To give some context to people who are unfamiliar with Indonesian medical education system, medical internship refers to a 1-year mandatory period for fresh medical graduates to serve in an assigned medical posts. This correspond to a one year, comprise of 6 months in a primary care facility (*puskesmas*/community health center) and 6 months in an acute care hospital. NTT is one of the 37 provinces in Indonesia, located in eastern part of Indonesia. Generally, the eastern part of Indonesia has seen slower economic growth compared to the western part. This roots to the economic epicentre located in Jakarta and Java, whereas logistical challenges emerge when distributing growth to other islands.

As the growth and development in this region is already left behind to its western counterparts, there are still many public health and economic problems surrounding this province, and by getting to deliver medical care in underdeveloped region in Indonesia, I hope could gain some sense of real life outside the ivory tower of capital and gain a sense of empathy of delivering care to underserved population.

The decision is set, and when the selection came, I got the post in Rote Ndao, one of the regency in East Nusa Tenggara.

Mind you, that Rote Ndao is not just any other region in the province. It is*the *southernmost region of Indonesia. I am closer to Darwin, North Australia, than to Jakarta. The region is very underdeveloped, one of the underdeveloped regions according to [the presidential decree](https://peraturan.bpk.go.id/Details/136563/perpres-no-63-tahun-2020). The health problems have been dire, with the rate of stunted growth and maternal mortality among the worst in Indonesia.

What surprised me even more was the fact that there were so many of my college mates who also would be serving here. Out of the 13 intern doctors, 11 were from my alma mater (UI). This was totally unprecedented because we did not plan to serve in this place altogether. Maybe a group of dyads, triads cliques that ensembled in this place together.

### About Rote

Rote Ndao is one of the regencies in East Nusa Tenggara. It is situated in the southwest of Timor Island, where the country Timor Leste is located. Being the southernmost region in East Nusa Tenggara, it also boasts the southernmost region in Indonesia. The word Rote Ndao is actually an equivalent word consisting of*Rote *(the main island) and*Ndao *(the lesser island situated west of Rote). However, Rote Ndao Regency actually consists of more than a hundred small islands, most of which are uninhabited.

The biggest island, Rote, is approximately 1.200 km² in area, similar to Bekasi Regency (1,224 km²) or Gresik Regency (1,911 km²). The number of population is only a bit less than 150,000 inhabitants. Bekasi having 20 times the population, and Gresik having 9 times more. But the disheartining fact is while Gresik and Bekasi has 19 and 40 hospital, respectively, Rote as a whole regency only has one.

And this is the hospital where I would serve for half a year.

![Ba’a Hospital Emergency Department (got from google search because I cannot recall any of my photo of this hospital).](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/800/0*PBlOqaI2EqTPjiZx.jpeg)

*Ba’a Hospital Emergency Department (got from google search because I cannot recall any of my photo of this hospital).*## First six months: hospital care in resource-limtied settings

For thirteen of us, we were split into two teams: one would have primary care rotation first and then the hospital rotation, whereas the other group would get the other way around. I happened to get the hospital rotation first.

There are certain rumours about the difference between internship experience in hospital and*Puskesmas*. Hospital rotation is known to be more clinically oriented, with most of day to day activities involve delivering medical care in certain hospital units, such as emergency department (ED), inpatient wards, and intensive care unit (ICU). On the other hand,*Puskesmas *rotation put lesser emphasis on clinical aspects of care, but also raising community engagement and public health activities. Most of my medical friends find this rather boring and tedious, but not for me (I will raise this further in the*puskesmas *section).

For this rotation, the District Health Office and the Hospital have kindly lent us a place to stay: an official residence. In this rather humble two-bedroom house, there lives seven of us intern doctors for the next six months. The master bedroom is inhabited by four male doctors; the smaller bedroom is filled with two female doctors. One guy decided to make a small corner in the living room as his quarter and continue to sleep there. There are only one bathroom for seven of us so you can imagine the pain in the morning waiting for shower. But the good thing is, we work in shifts anyway.

![The beautiful official residence.](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/800/1*XbWY2OlX4j8rTKxhUCgCKg.jpeg)

*The beautiful official residence.*And another very good thing: it is actually within the hospital complex. So commuting to work was never an issue. The shift starts at 8 AM and we usually depart by 7:59.

There are no reliable public transport in Rote, as far as I am aware. Just like most*Kabupatens *in Indonesia*,*most of people ride motorbike to anywhere — from as close as 5 minute walk to 3 hours of car ride. Walking to go somewhere seems out of norm, somehow. One time I walked to the nearest *warung*to get some stuff that is 10-minute walk away. One hospital staff with motorscycle happened to see me, approached me and offered me a lift.

To overcome this transport issue, the hospital lent us an old (but functional) motorcycle. We took turns in going anywhere far because it could only be 2 of us using the motorcycle at the same time (one as a passenger, of course).

This is the first time I actually learnt to ride a motorcycle. At first, it was much harder than riding a bicycle. I fell one time to a shallow ravine when getting around the village. But over time, I got used to it.

The first weeks of practicing medicine in Rote was quite rough. Most of Rote people do not speak regular Indonesian, but rather local dialect called *bahasa Kupang*. It is still Indonesian with some word differences and intonation. For example:*beta *or*be*- instead of*aku *or*saya *for ‘I/me’*. Sonde*instead of *tidak*for a ‘no’. For the people living in deep villages, they do not even speak *bahasa Kupang*. They speak bahasa Rote (*Rotenese*), which is a completely different language. Most people living in Ba’a, the Regency seat do not even speak that language. Sometimes I need translator from nurses or midwives to speak with certain patients from those remote areas.

Clinical wise, it is my first encounter between bridging best practice care that I received when I did my medical school (in the national referral hospital of Indonesia) to the practical reality of medical care in peripheral regions of Indonesia. The reality is far from perfect, especially here when the resources are very limited.

When I served the Emergency Department, many times I encountered patients already in bad condition. And when I asked why they did not come earlier (before it worsens), most of the time the patient’s family said it is because the hospital is so far away. Sometimes they need two to three hours of ride to reach the hospital. And most of the time they do not have a car. So they have to hire a pickup car to fetch them into a hospital (which cost quite a lot of money), or ask some neighbors to take them. They can call an ambulance (which I think is free of charge), but most of the time the symptoms already worsen so rapidly that waiting for ambulance akin to death sentence.

There are also a norm that whole extended family coming with the patient to the hospital, so you can see people having picnic outside the hospital. They are the patient’s family, patiently accompanying the patient, and praying them to get well.

There are also scarcity issue with medical equipment and supplies. I remember vividly one time there are epinephrine shortage in the whole hospital for a whole week. Epinephrine is an essential life-saving drug that is administered mainly during cardiac arrest. For it to be unavailable means we have to resort to much less effective drugs when encountering cardiac arrest patients, or worse: we did nothing.

![Ampoules of norepinephrine. At one time, this was the most sought object in the hospital.](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/800/1*hgTbYO858O-GUoCmyNmQHw.jpeg)

*Ampoules of norepinephrine. At one time, this was the most sought object in the hospital.*I also remember one time when there are and elderly man with a dislocated shoulder after falling off a ladder. External traction and repositioning were needed to get the shoulder back in place. Since the appropriate cloth that is needed to do the traction was unavailable, we ended up using an infant warmer drape to do the traction.

Power outage is more common than ever, averaged around twice per week. The good thing is the hospital have gasoline generator to power the essential services. But for extended power outage, the generator can only do so much. I remember that I have to accompany the obstetrician to complete the caesarean section with pitch black operating theater because the power was out during the operation. Fortunately, the power went out after the baby is delivered and continuing to close the incisions.

The real definition of*“tak ada rotan, akar pun jadi.”*### Facing mortalities

The limited healthcare resources, added with the poor access to the healthcare services culminates in what we fear — death.

During my six-month stint as an intern doctor in RSUD Ba’a, I vividly remember having to pronounce the death of 33 individuals.

Eleven of them was neonates, seven of them was children.

When you think it gets easier facing deaths in front of your eyes with time, you are completely wrong. It*never *gets easier. I felt emotionally heavier after facing a death of a patient, especially when I know it could have been the other way — either by more resources or access to them.

I vividly remember that one patient, a seventeen-year-old boy from East Rote, 2-hour drive from the hospital, came to the ED with a chief complaint of hallucinations. He was not fully conscious, not fully aware of his surroundings, and talked in jumbled, incoherent sentences. He was taken by his aunt’s family because his parents were in another island. They did not know what happened to him for the last couple of days because they were not with the patient until recently. Because of that, I could not get accurate medical history of the boy. The aunt said that before coming to the hospital, she took the patient to a*dukun *(shaman) because they thought the boy was possessed by an evil spirit.

But we do know now that it is not the case.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/600/1*r0ZgGpxaqRARUfr2gxfvKw.jpeg)

![I faced some bizarre vital signs here.](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/800/1*OM0v2TjAVEMox8Tpfbrknw.jpeg)

*I faced some bizarre vital signs here.*Just an hour after he came through the front door, his mental status worsen. He was then in somnolence, but still waking up through pain stimulus. And an hour after that, his blood pressure dropped rapidly. He had hypotension, and aggresive fluids and inotropics were given to maintain his blood pressure.

After further careful and improvised history taking to the nearest family member of the patient, it dawned that he had fever for about a week. That day was the seventh day he had chills.

It dawned upon us all the he was having a dengue shock syndrome. The hallucination, the hypotension, the fever — all suddenly made sense. But it was too late.

By then, he had cardiac arrest. And by 6pm — just three hours short — he was pronounced dead. Just a week ago, he was well and in good health just like anyone else.

It was one of the hardest night I had in Rote. I felt like I failed as a doctor. I felt I could save him, but at the same time I know the problem is bigger than this. I could be the most competent doctor in Indonesia that day but still his life could not be saved, anyway. A competent healthcare worker is only a small (still very important) part of the equation of a health system. How about the availability and access to the hospitals and clinics? How is the public health system? How to shift cultural belief to encourage people to take sick people to proper health professional instead of*dukun*?

It was my first real-world encounter to the famous ‘wicked’ public health problem. A problem that is so multipdisciplined and interlinked, we do not know where to start. But somewhere it is, we should start.

### Politicized medicine

Medicine is always political. But this time, I was able to see it with my own eyes.

I observe how hierarchy and social status in the community determine the quality and priority of care given by health professional. I was thought that public healthcare services should be given to the people regardless of status (should be based on triage), and fairness is uphold to every patient we have. But, perhaps I was too naive.

I am quite certain that most physicians and health professional have encountered this — certain patients demanding priority care because he or she is or is close relatives to someone important. In an established healthcare system, the ideal solution to meet this difficult patients is to refer to the standard operating procedure and keep going with the status quo. But in Rote, as in many parts of Indonesia, sadly those status and hierarchies*do *determine priority of care.

One day, I had a patient being closely monitored in the ED for worsening hemorrhagic stroke. He could not be admitted to the ICU because there is no specific plan that can be done here (no CT scan, no proper work up for any kind of stroke) so the plan was to closely monitor in the ED while educating the family about the possible workups.

One alternative is to refer to a bigger hospital. Since there is no other hospital in Rote, the only way forward is to transfer to nearest big hospital in Kupang, which is on another island. Now it is becoming a logistical problem. There are two ways to get out of this island, one is by sea (boat), or by air (plane). Local airport was not functional at that time so we can cross that one. Sea transport takes three and a half hours by ferry, which then takes a bout three hours of land travel by ambulance first and between port and the hospital. The schedule was also very limited, with the ferry only come once or twice daily, usually no later than 3pm.

We know that hemorraghic stroke is a ticking time bomb,*every *hour is vital. And the condition can worsen in a matter of minutes. The stakes are very high to transfer, and most of the time the patient’s family simply decide to take palliative treatment in the hospital while preparing for the eventual death. Perhaps, it was better to die honorably surrounded by family rather after scrappy cardiac life support in an ambulance on a rocking ship.

At the same time, the chief on duty of the ED was called by the hospital director. He said that a VIP was coming to the ED and request immediate and top quality care to the said person. He said he was also going to the ED himself to check on the incoming patient.

I thought this patient was having something really severe that needs immediate treatment. And there he finally came — it was a fucking vertigo. Fucking benign paroxysmal positional vertigo (BPPV) that can be treated with antihistamine and some rest to solve the dizzyness.

It was a condition that can easily wait another day to visit an outpatient clinic. And just because he was the head of a bank in the regency seat, we have to allocate the human resource that should be used to monitor and treat emergency patients (like the hemorraghic stroke) to treat him. It was almost unethical.

And I cannot put much blame on the director too. He was also a settler. It was just how things are run here. Power was displayed in such a vulgar manner. If you have connections or close to people in power, you*will *be served. If else, prepare to be ostracized, even worse — banished.

## Second six months: learning the community by delivering primary care

The second part of the internship is the primary care rotation. For the interns who have dealt with emergencies and severe patients for the past months, the shift into*Puskesmas *feels easier as it requires less stress and demanding schedule. However, as I deep further to the life of a*Puskesmas *doctor, the work was no less difficult. It was just different.

The key differences between secondary and primary care is this. In hospital, you treat patients. In Puskesmas, you treat people.

My puskesmas internship requires me to relocate further from the Regency seat, Ba’a, where the hospital is located. Me and the other three interns went to Delha, the region in the western part of the island.

West Rote is located about one and a half hour car ride away from Ba’a. And fortunately, the place is magnificent. It is perhaps the most touristic place in the whole Rote Ndao Regency. The flagship beach, Nemberala, is famous among die-hard surfers for its very big waves and pristine beaches.

The place is small, and even more secluded than Ba’a. There are substantially less people here. But there are a lot of hints of gentrification, with imposing luxury resorts made by European or American expatriates. It is an ironic feeling, where one of the most underdeveloped regions in Indonesia, there are pockets of luxury and wealth that are: 1) not distributed to locals; and 2) concentrated on settlers from high-income countries. It is one of the real-life lesson of international relations and dynamics that I encountered here in Rote.

With seclusion, comes logistical issues. With logistical issues, comes raised prices. The prices of groceries were higher here than in Ba’a. I could no longer buy petrol in petrol station, because there was only two petrol stations in the whole island, and both located in Ba’a. So we had to buy these bottled petrol resellers on roadsides, which even has higher prices than the resellers in Ba’a roadsides.

![A jerry can of gasoline.](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/800/1*Fanfm4xaYFY3ForsdoALNw.jpeg)

*A jerry can of gasoline.*In Kabupaten life, petrol is a liquid gold as mobility solely relies on motorized vehicle. Scarcity can easily result in unrest and thus, supply of petrol becomes cartelized to gain not only financial, but also political gain. I vividly remember when I sadly could not attend a ceremonial eid prayer during Eid al-Adha because that time there was petrol shortage for weeks and our motorcycles have run out of them. The nearest mosque was half an hour motorcycle ride away.

We, the four doctors, were lucky to be given a place to stay for the duration of the internship — another duty home inside the Puskesmas complex. It was quite decent with similar two bedroom, one story house. Because there was only four of us now, it iswas easier to share the space. Three male doctors (including me) sleep in the big bedroom, whereas the one female doctor sleep in the smaller bedroom.

Unfortunately, water pump is available for the houses within the Puskesmas complex. For the water supply, we have to rely on periodic refilling by clean water tanker truck to fill our house’s reservoir every 4 or 5 days. It costs about IDR 70,000 (approx. USD 4.2) for a single full-tank refilling.

Electricity was available to lighten our homes, though it comes with even more frequent outage than the one in Ba’a.

### Life as a primary care doctor

I thoroughly enjoyed the primary care rotation. I think it is a great opportunity for doctors to learn less about patients, but more about the people and the community we work in. If we liken the natural course of illness as a river, the primary care work is more upstream. We deal with healthier, more fit patients. The focus of care is less about curing ailments (although we still do it daily), but preventing them from being ill in the first place. This is where public health and medicine synergize, and I could not be more lucky to be in part of it.

Truth be told, life in the Emergency Department (or Hospital in general) can be disheartening. It is heart wrenching to go to pediatric wards every day and see terminally sick children, succumbed to Tuberculosis or severe acute malnutrition. On the other hand, the pediatric patients I met in Puskesmas were in much better condition. They still retain their energy, youth, innocence, with a bit of runny nose or nausea.

Puskesmas is an acronym for*Pusat Kesehatan Masyarakat *(trans. Community Health Center*).*It is a public primary care clinic in which all the care is borne by the government (so everyone can be treated, free of charge), but also playing additional role as public health centre that aims to improve the health of the community. Because of this dual focus, the medical equipment and supplies in the facility is even more scarce and minimal compared to the one in the Ba’a hospital.

What makes it hard is how this *Puskesmas*is the single health outpost for this very region. There is no other private clinic or health facility in West Rote. All health services in the region relies on this very institution. That is why this *Puskesmas*has a 24 hour ‘emergency’ service. I quote the emergency because it was not the conventional trauma center or emergency department where all the equipment and personnel is available. It was more that there are doctor and nurses stand by on duty 24/7. That is why even in this primary care rotation, we still work in shifts. We also happen to stay *literally*behind the *Puskesmas.*So it helped when there are urgent patients needing assistance, too.

Since the nearest hospital was one hour and half ride away, referring patient becoming not also in clinical but also logistical judgement. For things that can be treated *ad interim* and not life-threatening, we resorted to giving initial treatment and then referring the patients to hospital outpatient clinics so they can go by themselves.

And even if we gave greenlight for a referral, it is not easy from the patient side. Many such cases that patient or patient’s family refused to be referred to the hospital, though they might receive better and more comprehensive care there. The reason is various. First, as I have mentioned in the hospital chapter, when a patient go to the hospital, the whole extended family go with them. So the logistics and commitment from the family to accompany the patient downtown needs to be considered before agreeing for a referral. For economically disadvantaged patients, they might refuse because it will be to expensive for the family to go and stay in Ba’a, because the free ambulance can only take the patient and a couple of family members, while the rest must go by themselves, which cost liters of petrol.

Second, some from more traditional background refuse to receive conventional medical treatment. They thought all the relatives who were referred to the hospital eventually died. But we know what is really happening: patient’s family refused for referral until the patient condition worsen, until which they eventually relent and agrees to be referred, but with the patient’s condition already very severe. That is why most of the time, the referred patient ended up dying in the hospital.

### Connecting with people

Being a primary care doctor, it is not enough to be competent at clinical skills. I could argue it is more important to have cultural competence and engagement skills with the surrounding community. Having clinical skills no longer the sole leverage for our services, we have to be accepted in the community. That is why the everyday work of primary care doctor here involves a lot of talking, persuasion, house visits, meeting village chiefs, deliberations, empowering and motivating community health workers (*kader kesehatan*), and so on.

To be able to treat the community, we*have *to be a part of it first.

Difficult people comes in all forms: uncooperativeness, machiavellian tendencies, corruption and greed, or even outright psychopathy. The thing with peripheral region is there is not a lot of people around here. Labour market is not working as it is intended, certain posts cannot be easily replaced. Hence, as it is with equipment, we had to rely on the people we have here. And there could be a great variety of people, both deviating to the better, and the worse.

Politics is even more serious business in villages. Since there is not a lot of business and employment opportunities to this place, much of money circulation circulates around government officials, who have to govern the place. I think this is a common occurence in*Kabupatens *after all. So, political race for public posts such as Village Chief (*Kepala desa*) and Village Secretary (*Sekretaris desa*) are becoming as cutthroat as ever. Factions that formed from families are made, racing for the chair by establishing coalitions and black campaigns to belligerent political opponents. It was a winner-takes-all game, where winning the post means securing significant funding such as Village Fund (*dana desa*) which can amounted to billions of Rupiah. One family could own a resort and kiosk, racing against another family who control the petrol distribution in West Rote and main supplier of clean water facility. Observing this in real time is like watching microcosm of my own country.

During election year, colors were as taboo as sex and drugs. Wearing certain colors can imply allegiance to certain political party, which may support or be against the party that was supported by the ruling Village government. This is why Puskesmas staffs werestrongly enforced to wear neutral color during election years. Or in certain times, they might need to wear*certain *color to imitate allegience to specific party. I will not elaborate this further.

The seclusion, dealing with difficult people, and hard work environment makes psychological strain between us interns were inevitable. It also did not help that out of all 13 interns, 4 of who were here were the most stubborn of them all. Small (or big) altercations and quarrel did happen. But looking back to that moment, it was hard for all of us, and we just wanted to keep the ship afloat.

### Life outside Puskesmas

Less work hours in primary care (compared to hospital) means more time to do leisure, like enjoying sunset on the beach every afternoon, jogging on beachside, swimming in Loedi beach (perhaps the*best *beach I have ever encountered), and venturing around the island by motorcycle.

The puskesmas people were full of good vibes as well. During night shifts, if there was no patients we liked to play cards. We set a projector behind the puskesmas to watch Champions League finals (it was City against Inter, Inter won by 1–0). The sky is so much clearer in remote areas like here so on clear days we just stargazed. It was life full of kind people and beautiful nature.

The sense of caring from the people of is so genuine that I could not find it in other places. I owe so much of my mental wellbeing to my housemates, the other interns, and the neighbours, especially the people who live in the Puskesmas complex. Although we were newcomers to the place and living only for six months, they welcomed us and always making sure we are well settled.

On music, I deeply resonate with Kurt Vonnegut, in which he said

> “Music is, to me, proof of the existence of God. It is so extraordinarily full of magic, and in tough times of my life I can listen to music and it makes such a difference.”

People who know me already know how much I enjoy listening and playing music. And that liking did not attenuate when I was in Rote. Instead, I used it as a emotional camera for important moments. Photographic camera can capture the light that enters a place in a particular time, whereas music capture the emotions and the energy at that particular moment.
