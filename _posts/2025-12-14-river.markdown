## “Why London?”

I had never been to the UK. Heck, I had never been to Europe. My sole exposure to the so-called western civilization had been only through mass media and the internet.

I have been dreaming of seeing the world more. It is true that I am fortunate enough to live in such a connected era, in which air travel is possible and flow of information is hyperaccelerated through internet. I could see how London or New York neighborhood looks like from Youtube videos, or learning about French culture by downloading an e-book from the internet.

Why London? Honestly, I did not know initially. Imperial is my second choice. My initial first choice for my master’s was Karolinska Institutet in Sweden. My third choice was University of Copenhagen in Denmark. I wanted to study public health in these scandinavian countries because I admire social democratic countries that can provide welfare to its people.

Why Imperial then? The question is yet to be answered. United Kingdom is not a part of the scandinavian circle, after all. Hence, it is a simple reason that Imperial has a very strong public health programme, with strong expertise in infectious disease epidemiology — one area that I have deep interest in. There are other strong public health schools in the UK, say the London School of Hygiene and Tropical Medicine, or Oxford. But for me, Imperial had the most suitable programme that suited my knowledge gaps, according to my understanding at that time.

During my time in Rote, I had been mentally prepared to study in Stockholm. I was learning a bit of Swedish and getting to know its culture. I read some books on Swedish culture in which I found a endearing. I put the Karolinska campus as my phone wallpaper. I hoped it could be the reality for the next year.

But the reality said otherwise. I was put into the waiting list for my application to KI. Not exactly a rejection, but not an admission either. I was also denied admission for the Global Health programme at UCPH (Copenhagen). Gladly, Imperial gave me an offer. Since this is the only acceptance letter I had, I wanted to make the best use of this opportunity.

And one thing led to another. It turns out that my junior high pals — Kafi and Aji — also received offers from universities in London. We made a group chat to find accomodations together. And while there have been challenges in finding place to stay, we were able to secure a place — a 3-bedroom flat at Finchley Road NW3 — which inadvertently became one of the highlights of our time in London.

## Preparation

Preparing my departure from Rote was not an easy feat. The internet connection in Rote was so bad, and I have to deal with frequent power outage during my attempts to apply for visa or discussing accomodation candidates with Aji and Kafi. The only viable visa appointment location was in Jakarta, which is a bit ironic because it is only 10-minute drive away from my home in Jakarta. Requesting time off during my internship was tricky, because this program only allows three days off for the whole period. So I had to take extra shifts so other interns could fill my shift during my time away back to Jakarta.

And it is sort of impossible to take only three days off from Rote. There was only one schedule of departure from Rote to Kupang by boat every day and it was in late morning, while the flight from Kupang to Jakarta was usually only once and it was the early morning (like 6 AM), so you have to stay the night before the flight.

But with deliberate planning and some extra shifts, I could manage to take more than a week off for a trip to Jakarta to attend my visa application appointment and predeparture training from my scholarship programme.

LPDP Scholars are mandated to attend predeparture training (called*Persiapan Keberangkatan*). Before the five-day offline training in Jakarta, we were assigned some group assignments that eventually aims to bond us and getting to know other scholars (I think this is a normal practice in many scholarship programs). Somehow I became the medical coordinator for my batch, ensuring the safety and health of the Scholars during the intense training (starting from from dawn to near midnight, everyday).

One of the key takeaways from the training was a remark from Yanuar Nugroho, a well-known Indonesian scholar and member of the LPDP’s advisory board. He said that Indonesian scholars should not only spend the time abroad studying, but also travel around and enjoy their life. This is surprising, knowing the the general public opinion toward this has been overwhelmingly negative, as it was commonly seen as ‘abusing the taxpayer’s money’ (LPDP is funded through a an endownment fund in which the government budget could contribute to it).

His reasoning is apt. Studying only in the classroom is limiting. Experiencing a different life than the one back home is also part of learning. He said that learning to adapt to different norms makes us more emphatetic to people that are different with us.

He openly despised those Indonesian students who only mingle with other Indonesians abroad, instead of building diverse network from different countries. It is about time that a country with fourth biggest population in the world participate more in the global discourse, as it was famously known as the ‘biggest invisible nation’.

And the best thing is these places can be easily visited because of how well connected the city is. London has an integrated public transport system, called Transport for London, which comprises of mass rapid transit system (called London Underground or tubes), buses (the famous double decker red bus), overground trains, trams and DLRs. All of these services are integrated with one faring system. So, with a daily, weekly, or monthly passes, I can easily access all of these means. And the stops are practically everywhere in greater London. I am almost never farther than a 15-minute walk (heck, even 10) away from the nearest bus stop or tube station in London.

That way, I could plan my travel ahead with greater reliability, so I could visit multiple places in a day. This cannot be done in Jakarta, where the urban mobility still relies on road-based personal vehicle or private transport services (such as ride hailing motorcycle like ojek or taxis). Traffic jams are predictably unpredictable. We always know traffic jams do happen in Jakarta streets, but the length and severity differ every time, so we cannot accurately predict our commuting time. I had never realised how limited my mobility was in Jakarta until I lived in London.

## First term: getting started

Enough of the leisure, I had to do what I am being paid for here — to study. I arrived at the Imperial main campus in South Kensington, I was struck in awe. I will be studying* *in one of the best universities in the world. The place is full of history and legacy. Sir [Alexander Fleming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_Fleming), was an Imperial academic when he first formulated penicilin — the very first antibiotic in the world. Imperial School of Public Health was also played a key role during the Covid-19 Pandemic through Imperial Covid-19 task force, in which they famously published the [Report № 8 on Nonpharmaceutical interventions for Covid-19 pandemic](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/technical-report-on-the-covid-19-pandemic-in-the-uk/chapter-8-non-pharmaceutical-interventions) (i.e. the first report mentioning the social distancing and ‘lockdown’). [MRC Global Infectious Diseases Analysis](https://www.imperial.ac.uk/mrc-global-infectious-disease-analysis/), one of the research groups in Imperial College School of Public Health, has played a key role as strategic partner with World Health Organization in various outbreak responses.

I was also deeply honored to study public health in the very place where modern public health was born. The field of modern public health and epidemiology might be practically born after the famous Cholera outbreak in SoHo in 1847, in which John Snow applied the use of geospatial analysis in mapping the cause of the cholera based on the distribution of patients (it turns out it is because of contaminated water source). The replica of the contaminated pump in Broad Street is still displayed there.

The first term in Imperial MPH is for core modules: every student had to take it. I learned the foundations of public health, statistics, epidemiology, and also research methods in this term. What I like about the teaching is how foundational the curriculum is. Before studying more complex derivative of a subject, e.g., epidemiology, one had to have solid foundation on operational definition in the very basic concepts of epidemiology such as incidence rate, odds ratio, and confounding. This in turn really helped me with understanding more complex topics by myself, because the core concepts had been taught and verified by the experts from the School.

One session that really shaped my thinking towards public health is a question by Dr Richard Pinder, the lecturer for Foundation of Public Health Practice module, with his simple question to the whole class in our first meeting.

> “What is the difference between health and health care?”

We were not aware of the difference. If there is, maybe the simple thought that health is a state, whereas health care is a service.

But his answer reflected how we, as public health students, differentiate them:

> “Healthcare happens when health fails.”

People outside of public health career (including me) mistakenly used both term interchangeably when talking about ‘health’ sector. But when someone is talking about*health *sector, he or she is more likely to talk about healthcare sector: hospitals, pharmaceuticals, rehabilitative services. These are services that happen when ‘health’ fails.

So that is where the clear distinction between public health and medicine are made. This field is the upstream of the same river. Whereas medicine is the science and art of treating the sick, public health is the science and art of keeping people healthy.

It was also very exciting to study public health and engage with the world-renowned experts on the topic. So I used this very valuable opportunity to engage and ask many questions as possible while I was there.

There was this small scientific gathering on global health in the University of Cambridge in which I am eventually register and attended. It was a routine event every three months, nothing really special. It was also free to attend so “why not,” I said to myself.

But that meeting turned out really enlightening with every speaker and discussion were about key current challenges in global health. I was amazed not only by the quality of the presentation*content*(which is kind of expected since these academics were affiliated with Cambridge, King’s, and other top institutions), but also the*delivery*. Professor Richard Sullivan, an urologist who also a scientist and advisor on humanitarian health gave what I thought the most compelling lecture I have ever witness with my own eyes. It was about the contemporary issues of healthcare delivery in war zones and the concept of ‘therapeutic geographies’. The concepts were really interesting to learn, Professor Sullivan was also very engaging in his presentation, and the delivery was also very understandable for me who is at most a novice in this very field.

The lecture is*so good *that I emailed one of the course organizer in my MPH program to invite him to give lectures to other students, and she welcomed the idea since he is also her scientific collaborator.

The other presenters were also present their work very well. One talked about the philogenetic tree on*Aedes aegypti *mosquitoes (the ones that transmit Dengue fever) and also on the application of artificial intelligence (AI) on noncommunicable diseases. I can see how they are really passionate with their work and believe that their work contribute positively to the society.

The icing of the cake was the keynote lecture at the end of the meeting on the importance of rethinking health for the education outcomes. It was delivered by Sir [Leszek Borysiewicz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leszek_Borysiewicz) (he preferred to be called Boris). He is a quite a big deal, previously the president of Cancer Research UK and also Vice Chancellor of the University of Cambridge. However, he was so humble in his presentation and delivery. He uttered self-depreciating jokes throughout the presentation and basically delivered what should be a complex topic in such a simple manner.

Previously, I was never that much inspired when listening to an academic lecture. It was usually just dry, objective, and aloof. But that meeting really changed the way I look into scientific communication. I can see that they really care about their work, putting their heart into it, and they showed empathy in communicating it so every person in the audience could understand. And they were really humble about themselves and their work that they 1) were easy to approach in between sessions and even debate their work; 2) welcomed the idea that they might wrong about their very subject.

I said to myself: this is how scientists should behave. I had enough interacting with many academics back home (or even here) that were so prideful of their credentials that they refused to engage with students and early career scientists, let alone discussing and debating their work in great detail. The mindset should be not about being the gatekeeper of truth, but the pursuer of it.

While I thought it was a small event, but God knows how many things I learned during that day.

## Second term: elective modules and exploring the world

In the second term, it was the time for elective modules. I have been interested in taking the Global Health stream, so I took many global health and health policy classes. More than that, I also took classes on health impact assessment and health system development and financing.

It was a lot of fun. The global health classes was so enlightening and sheds light on the contemporary issues of global health governance such as the role of international organization and emerging role of non-state actors (such as donor foundations and trusts). Health impact assessment is the module in which I feel really underqualified for because I was very unfamiliar with environmental health science. I thought the module is more about general health impact assessment as in drafting report and measurement plan on health impacts towards projects or policies, but it turns out it is especailly tailored for environmental health setting, that is measuring health impact modelling for environmental insults such as air pollution, climate change, and urban planning.

It was my encounter with the field of air pollution study and how sophisticated the current development of knowledge is. About the air quality guidelines, measuring PM10 concentration using sensors, and estimating health outcomes such as all-cause mortality using health impact modelling (such as AirQ+ programme by WHO). The false image of this module opened me up into a field that I have little to no knowledge on it, and as far as I felt like a deer in a headlights when attending these classes, I was very grateful to eventually underwent this module.

And the key highlights of this term was the health economics. This was the field that I had been interested in a long time, but I did not have the guts to choose this as my Master’s degree course. When I entered undergraduate college, I was accepted to both accounting and medicine major in the university I am eventually enrolled in, and I chose Medicine. This field enabled me to return to my initial interest in economics and how it can be beneficial for the public health.

And yes, it was a gruesome and challenging module. I think it was fair, because everyone got their fair share of headache. Those with health and medicine background would find it difficult in understanding economic concepts, while students with economics background would find it hard to understand health and medical concepts. Its inherent multidisciplinary aspect is what makes this subject so appealing to me, and as what a wise person said: you cannot compete with someone who is having fun.

It has also what I think the hardest final module assignment in the whole degree outside of final dissertation: a 10-page economic evaluation study, made from scratch. It was a group work, so good team dynamics were essential in completing this monumental work. But gladfully, I am blessed with such highly motivated and cooperative groupmates. So we could finish it, have great grades, and even [presented it in the ISPOR Europe](https://www.valueinhealthjournal.com/article/S1098-3015(24)03640-4/fulltext) in Barcelona, Spain.

## Term 3: exploring the world (and dissertation)

The third term was the most dynamic and independent phase of my master’s study. It is the ‘*make it or break it*’ phase as it is completely down to the students on how do we wanted it to be like. There are no classes, only optional tutoring for those who needed it. The whole term was dedicated fully for research project — that is the Dissertation.

For this final project, I was so lucky to secure an ongoing project on estimating the cost of illness of malaria, blend of communicable diseases and health economics — two of my most favorite public health topics. I was lucky to do this project under research fellows of MRC Global Infectious Diseases Analytics itself, which I mentioned earlier. And for the next three to four month since April, I had been working on this.

Library is the real MVP for this term. Luckily for Imperial students, we could choose multiple libraries in spreaded Imperial campuses to do our independent studying and work. There is South Kensington campus library (called the Abdus Salam Library), the biggest of them all. And then there is also St. Mary’s Library located in St. Mary’s campus in Paddington. And there is also Charing Cross library in Hammersmith, to name a few. I have visited them all but my absolute favorite is the St. Mary’s one. It has been the indirect witness of my struggle in completing the dissertation, watching me clock in 10 am and clock out 10 pm almost every day trying out new models and writing my dissertation.

The reason why this is my favorite is multiple. First: the place is majestic with the open balcony for reading room and wood panels. ### First oral presentation experience

Never came across my mind that I would do my first scientific oral presentation at University of Oxford. I had never done this before, not even the ones back home in Indonesia. It was started when I put great concern on the frequent attack on healthcare workers in conflict zones, be it in Palestine, Ukraine, Sudan, and other places. I have great interest in humanitarian health, and thinking about becoming a humanitarian doctor in the future. So we initiated this small research group with Ara and Tsania to critically review this issue from a scientific standpoint.

We were just trying our luck in submitting this into a conference in Oxford. Being accepted for poster presentation in such a presigitious event in itself was already an achievement. Little did we know that we ended up being accepted for oral presentation. More than that, our presentation was put in the main conference hall, the same podium that is used for keynote presentations. We were so proud of ourselves in achieving this. However, suddenly the stakes were becoming higher, as I have to present this in front of world renowned experts in global health and bioethics.

We were preparing this presentation between our studies, did several meetings. I also consulted with one of the Imperial faculty who happen to have practical experience and expertise on this topic (she is a infectious disease physician and humanitarian worker — talking about personal idol!)

I saw the list of participants, the inferiority complex jumped in even more. It was full of experts from all over the world, from Oxford itself, WHO, even OUCRU, the place I currently work now. The most novice participants before me were PhD students. I felt really out of place and thinking about quitting before I embarrass myself on stage.

But Ara and Tsania encouraged me to carry on. And carrying on, I did. Surprisingly, the audience really paid attention to my presentation. There are a lot of questions. Even Patricia Kingori, one of the renowned sociologist from Oxford University gave a thoughtful reflection on the topic I brought. It was really a nice and warm ending to the anxiety of preparing it. I was reminded that in a true scientific forum, every one has a right of voice, no matter who he or she is.

## The joy of the mundane things

Since London has glaring reputation for its sunny days, I had no other choice to bring my clothes to the laundromat, which happen to be 15 minute walk from my flat.
