### “Greetings from the World Health Organization”

**LONDON **— It was late August of 2024. I sat on my bed, looking looking outside the window of a Sunday evening while thinking:

> “this will be over soon.”

In two weeks, I would present my dissertation. After that, is basically a wrap. The degree would be conferred online anyway, and the graduation ceremony will be next year.

My visa ended in January 2025, but my rent contract was due in mid-September, so I had to find somewhere else to stay should I wanted to extend my stay in London.

Living in London is very expensive, everyone knows that. My flat rent alone was more than four times the minimum wage of Jakarta. When the study completed, the financial support from the scholarship would end too. Based on my savings at that time, I could survive about a month in London without a job, but having to scramble to find a place to stay. Those awkward month- or two-month long sublet is a bit tricky to find.

It is the aspiration for almost all international students to stay another year or two abroad to build a career in the region they studied. Countless job applications had been made, all without any leads whatsoever. From hundreds of applications I made*,*it only led me to a virtual interview, which I blew, anyway.

And it was already late, the rent was due in weeks. Time was ticking. I had to make my decision. As far as job hunt here has been disappointing, I had to be realistic. Extending my stay in London would add little chance of getting a job. Some people wait until almost a year (or even beyond) before securing a role. Career market has been very bad, it is not looking very good from a prospective graduate (or in cynical term, soon-to-be unemployed). That common wisdom, thinking that studying in a reputable college would help you secure job easily, does not ring with me personally.

But the tide suddenly changed. As I was exploring the flight tickets back home to Jakarta, a notification came to my email.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/800/1*duGiHppFoac04Gzn2N9tVQ.png)

I quickly stood, silently looking out the window. The phone screen was still on. It showed that I was invited for an online interview for an internship position at the World Health Organization.

I was so shocked that I could not say a word. I never thought that I could pass the screening phase. I knew for a fact that an internship for any UN Agency has always been very competitive, let alone WHO — which is pratically every global health student’s dream employer.

The adrenaline then rushed in. I was elated with utter joy and relief. The confidence boost that this gave me could not be underestimated. I was feeling very hopeless in the job hunt. But now, the fact that I was shortlisted for an interview for this very role, gave me hope that my efforts were not lost cause.

At that time, I did not even think about the chance of actually accepted after the interview. The fact that I got invited gave me closure that I needed before I head back home.

So I prepared for that interview. The role I applied was about neglected tropical diseases, one of the global health topics that I have deep interest in. The interview eventually took less than an hour.

The announcement came a week after. I got the offer.

And the rest is what this writing will be about — what I think is the most significant 6 months of my life, so far.

For the final chapter of my so-called*extended public health training*, I headed directly into the center of global health governance itself — the World Health Organization in Geneva, Switzerland.


I spent about two months back home in Jakarta between the end of my study and the start of the internship. I rekindled with my family and friends after one year long away abroad. At the same time, I also said goodbye because I will be away again, at least a while.

Same with securing the internship, the preparation for staying in Geneva has also been riddled with luck. I heard from other people that real estate market is even more cutthroat in Geneva. This city hosts the record number of international organization headquarters, so international staffs come and go in daily basis. Advertisement for an apartment can go about three days before it is being sold to other people.

The worst part: I had no one to consult to. I knew barely anyone there, except my soon-to-be supervisor and the current intern who I corresponded only by email. Different to my previous experience in Rote and London, I had no other people that I can lean into. This time, I was all by myself.

Oh and I barely spoke French.

But after some hopeless queries in the glocals.com (english-written social platforms for international staffs in Geneva that also posts some classifieds), I could secure a room in a shared apartment: the one that was inhabited by the current intern.

I was planning to stay in hostels for like a month while seeking for accomodation there, but turns out I did not need to do that.

So all was set. Visa (thankfully, no visa application drama), lodging, and plane ticket (paid by the Organization).

I went with my tenths of my college mates during my flight to Rote, with 5 of my Indonesian pals to London. But this time, I went to Geneva all by myself.

## Winter (December 2024 — February 2025)

### Arrival at Geneva

It was 28 November 2025, seven in the morning, Central European Time. It was rather cold, unlike the one time I went here in June (*no shit*). The sky was grey, and I brought my two big suitcases straight into bus that took me to my lodging for the next 24 weeks.

Because of my poor planning and refusal to book a taxi (because it would cost an arm and a leg), I ended up dragging my 30 kilogram suitcases*each *in a twenty minute walk between the bus stop to my apartment. The pavement is different than the ones in London. It is much smaller, and literally it is only an elevated asphalt at the peripheries of the road. This makes all the suitcase dragging much less convenient and a bit awkward when another pedestrian comes to my way (I took up the whole pavement with my two suitcases).

But somehow, I arrived in this subletted apartment. An aging man greeted me, he is the owner of the apartment, and he lives there too. He introduced me to the other roommates. I was given some brief tour to his house.

The apartment, without any overexaggeration, is perhaps the nicest apartment that I ever stayed in. It is a four-bedroom penthouse in a medium-rise apartment complex in Lancy, just the outskirts of the core*Ville de Genève*.

### Adjusting

I spent the first couple of days orienting myself to the environment and recovering from the jet lag. I visited again the lake, the downtown, and looking at the international organization headquarters along the way. Admittedly, Geneva in the fall or winter was much less exciting than in the spring and summer. But it was still a very nice place.

I also bought groceries, in which I had my first culture shock. You had to look at my face when I saw the prices of the groceries in Coop Switzerland for the first time. The prices are absolutely bonkers.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/600/1*7-hHJlNlkDG9PFegUury_Q.jpeg)

![18 CHF for a pound of beef and 5 CHF for a bag of chips. For a moment I thought Swiss people defecate gold because what are these prices?](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/600/1*_Qzs9hi8r7oZil4kh1vpVQ.jpeg)

*18 CHF for a pound of beef and 5 CHF for a bag of chips. For a moment I thought Swiss people defecate gold because what are these prices?*

I thought London is already expensive as it is, but Geneva is just on another level. A basket of groceries that I could buy for GBP 20 at Sainsbury’s in London would easily cost twice as much here. And then I got reminded again with the list of most expensive cities in the world:

![Cost of living city ranking 2025 (Mercer)](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/800/1*cSW9mzFVIkNEB-79lnYOYw.png)

*Cost of living city ranking 2025 (Mercer)*Looking at my allowance from WHO, which was about the same amount with the allowance I had back in London, I would need to be even more frugal with my spending. No splurging, prioritizing on roof on top (rent) and food in the fridge. For personal entertainment, I would just utilized the common goods like visiting parks and museums, strolling around the city, and perhaps buying train tickets to travel around the country.

The second culture shock is silence. One thing I observed when I first stepped into the apartment in Geneva was how tranquil it was. It was so silent that faint bare footsteps on wooden parquet on my apartment can be heard very clearly. Back home in Jakarta, as silent as you can go, you may hear the sound of air conditioner or people talking outside. Or in London, the sound of traffic at the front of the flat window. Maybe it was a mix of cutting edge soundproofing and the culture of keeping things low.

Switzerland has a specific law regulating quiet hours. It was mandated by law that you have to be quiet between 10pm and 6am every weekday, and basically the whole day for Sundays. The law is so strict that you can not even flush your toilet at that time. Swiss people really appreciate tranquility, in which it also grows on me.

Talking about Swiss people, it brings me to the third culture shock on understanding how people behave here. To give an impression, it is the polar opposite with Indonesians. From what I observe, Swiss people do not like small talks, reserved by default, and very hard to make friends with (I read somewhere that most of them already built their small social circle since school days and not interested in expanding them after reaching adulthood). This is very much different from Indonesians which tend to be very easy going and warm, and making close connections in every facet of life.

Unlike Indonesians, Swiss people has an almost pedantic sense of dutifulness. This means they are ardent law abider by the letter and will gladly reproach those who do not comply with this custom. This I actually can get very much behind because I think it is the foundation of high trust society. This is really shown in the form of punctuality. Geneva public transport system is punctual by the minute, 90% of the time. In workplace, it is the norm to come*and *leave work on time. As someone who yearns a sense of fairness in my own country, to live like this has been a breath of fresh air.

### Beacon of civilization

My initial impression of the Swiss people in general is they are reasonable. This might sound underwhelming but I meant it as a huge praise. With all nonsensical things that I saw in Jakarta, Rote, London, and all other places, I saw the Swiss people as the ones who*figured it out*. They have quality education system and built a society that respect the rules of authority. On the other hand, they prevent the rise of despotism by exercising one of the few direct democracy in federal and cantonal level (hence I saw many pamphlets and posters of*oui *and*non, *which is a campaign to support or resist a certain referendum).

![You will see these kinds of poster a lot in Switzerland. Rather than ugly flags of political parties, they adorned the public spaces to encourage political participation. The posters are well designed too!](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/800/1*cef5zQWmjZLlunD9VH7blg.jpeg)

*You will see these kinds of poster a lot in Switzerland. Rather than ugly flags of political parties, they adorned the public spaces to encourage political participation. The posters are well designed too!*The people are quite industrious by European standard, but still very much respecting work life balance. They overall earned a lot, enough to support exorbitant living cost in their own country, while the majority of them do not spend their money for extravagant things (you know, the things you see in Jakarta, London, or Dubai, for example). Instead, they spent it for what*matters*, leisure time, access to nature (Swiss are ardent hikers) and high-quality groceries. This I think is the ideal blueprint for financial stability. If some day in the future I earn enough, this should be the way I live.

Swiss chocolate, unironically, is one of the great examples of Swiss ethos. During my time in Geneva, I had consistently bought different chocolate bars I could find every week. In every convenience store they all have this dedicated shelf containing refined Swiss chocolate bars that I must spend at least 10 minutes analysing which one to buy. And it was all otherworldly.

![Shelf of goodness](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/800/1*ejI8smx19CkeualqvqIYPw.jpeg)

*Shelf of goodness*And what makes Swiss chocolate so great? It is not because they give the biggest portion (most are regular-sized and thin). Not also because they are the sweetest (most of the time I bought dark chocolate, but even the milk chocolates are not as sweet as the ones in other countries). Most of their chocolates do not have those gimmicky fillings or toppings (although they do have Lindt Dubai Chocolate, after all). But what makes them great is simply perfecting the*balance*of the cocoa, milk, sugar, and other ingredients into one coherent unit that is delightful to consume. It is not by being ultra-competitive and being over-the-top, but by being refined for a recipe so simple yet so difficult to perfect.*That *is the Swiss way, and I think only in Japan that I see a similar ethos.

As much as this is the ideal way to live, we have to understand the underlying context on how the Swiss reach this stage. For so long, Switzerland has been an independent state for centuries. Utilizing its geographical location to become buffer state that separate great powers throughout European history, Switzerland is practically untouched and has been a beacon for neutrality (joined the UN only in 2002 and never joined the EU). Consequently, they used this advantage to become a reliable intermediary, be it in commodity trade, finance, and banking.

What I saw here on the surface of Switzerland stability and wealth is the accumulation of comparative advantage in the regional economy that has been passed from generations to generations for more than four centuries long. In short, this is what old money nation looks like. And they can be civilized and reasonable only after affording this lifestyle for so long. This is my first direct encounter with nation-wide wealth, not merely at individual level.

### Loneliness

For all the exciting things that happened within the first weeks of living in Geneva, I must also share the not-so-fun parts of it. Loneliness had been a central theme basically for the first three months of my internship. Perhaps I have shared this experience to my closest friends, but it really is one of the toughest part of living there.

There are many factors, I think, that exacerbates this feeling. First, my unfamiliarity with French. I figured that I adapted more quickly to the life in London the year before (although I never stepped foot in Europe before) partly because I am already familiar and fluent with the local language. To live in a country in which you do not speak the language well bring additional level of isolation. It is different with when we visit countries that we do not speak the language. We usually visit the touristy parts of the place and those parts are filled with English-speaking vendors and people.

Sure, Geneva hosts the world record number of International Organizations HQs in the world and it is also one of the financial center of the continental Europe, so the vibe is already international by default. Also, my landlord who have lived here for more than forty years said that Geneva is ‘*the most compact metropolis’*, for a city so small (not even bigger than Camden), it showcases as much diversity in nationalities and ethnicities as in New York or Berlin. But still, there is certain dread in using English when interacting with locals, as French-speaking communities have notorious reputation of ignoring people who speak English to them, or simply despise foreigners who attempted to speak bad French. AFrench catch-22, if you will.

But again, the fastest way to learn a language is to live in a place where the people speak the language you want to learn. Second, after a couple of months I simply stopped giving a shit and speak in English if my very-limited French comes to a dead end(that means, follow up questions after «*comment ça va*?» «*très bien, et toi*?» «*je vais très bien, merci*.»

More than that, I was truly alone. I knew nobody here, I reach every network I had that had a glimpse of experience in this city to ask for advice on how to survive in this city. During the end-year holidays, I actually despise the fact that WHO mandated a sweeping holiday for all employees between Christmas to New Year, because my only source of network was the coworker I got to know in my first weeks. Outside of the office, I knew no one.

There were days where I could spend a whole day not uttering a single word from my mouth. I remember when I visited Vevey in January, I was sitting on a bench facing the Giant Fork in Lake Geneva. I opened my lunch box, which contains an already freezing cold*Nasi Goreng *I made the day before (I have to prep my meals because I had not enough money to buy food during the trips). When I was enjoying this cold fried rice (because I had not eaten anything since breakfast and the day has been full of me walking around the Romandie), an old Swiss man wishing «*bon appétit*» to me as he walked in front of me.

![La Fourchette de Vevey, Suisse.](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/800/1*PyX0cXWHM25JfpT3cSewvw.jpeg)

This loneliness was so bad that my initial impression about this internship experience was: 1) I do not like it here; 2) I do not want to be in a profession that requires me to be in solitude for extended period of time; 3) No matter what luxury that this country can bring to me, it will never replace the genuine human connection that I sought.

### Turns of tides

All went well from the HQ at the start. Interns got to meet the Director General, Dr. Tedros, in my second week of internship, and year-end celebration were still festive and warm. Still, there had been rumours of what would come. An imminent threat: Donald Trump had just won the election.

![Meeting Dr. Tedros Adhanom Ghebreyesus — Director General of the World Health Organization.](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/800/1*4EStBOc-ljkaein2dU4lig.jpeg)

*Meeting Dr. Tedros Adhanom Ghebreyesus — Director General of the World Health Organization.*Since his campaign days, Trump has been rallying his supporters with commitment to curb multilateralism, in which he promised to withdraw United States from the World Health Organization membership. Why? He mentioned how ‘inefficient’ the organization is and how the underwhelming response in managing the Covid-19 pandemic. He also mentioned other reasons — including fairness of assessed contributions by countries (in which he deemed China’s contributions as too low).

And then it happened. At the same time with the his inauguration as the 47th President of the United States (20 January 2025), Trump signed an executive order to withdraw from the WHO. This was (still kind of is) a big deal not only diplomatically. United States*was *the biggest donor of the Organization by a long margin. The repercussions of this action was the entire shake-up within the organization as it faced the most dire funding crisis since its inception in 1948.

I saw the historical decline of global health in real time, from the epicentre of its governance.

Within weeks, all US counterparts within the Organization was furloughed. Strategic partners such as US Central Diseases and Control and Prevention (CDC), Food and Drug Administration (FDA), and other health related US agencies closed communications with the Organization. All of the USAID and USAID-funded programmes and initiatives were freezed and shut down. Other US-funded global health organization such as Gavi and The Global Fund faced major funding cuts (if not funding halt altogether).

This is a major defeat for multilateralism and global cooperation. Hundreds of millions of lives are at risk with many of this public health and care-strengthening programmes are being stopped, especially in developing countries. From me personally, my entire career trajectory is at risk, as this trend means there will be scarcity of career opportunities in this very field.

On the other hand, this is also a wake up call for the global health field that is getting complacent. The flow of aid structure has been built on unsustainable structure for so long, in which the entire sector was build ‘on the mercy of the superpower countries’. International development has been more contaminated by strategic plan to exert soft-power, rather in empowering beneficiaries. Even in the implementation itself, the international development has caused disproportionate accumulation of wealth among those who exercise the programs rather who should benefit from it.

The road to this defeat has been paved with good intentions, after all. The world changes so quickly, it is the matter of time for global health to change, too.

## Spring (March-May 2025)

### Building human connection

That Trump order taught me about an important thing in life: no matter how hard your own endeavour in achieving your goal, there are still some things that are beyond our own control. Nobody prepared me that the very field I am planning to build a career in will be in flames right after I joined WHO a an intern. Just like Southeast Asian financial crisis in 1998, or Covid-19 Pandemic in 2020, we are part of collective history in which black swan, an unpredictable event that changes the whole trajectory of history and challenges the assumption of status quo, occurs.

Among other things, safe to say that I had adapted quite well with Geneva life (according to my own standard). I became accustomed with the quietness, the punctuality, the pedantry, also majority of the norm. I could then shift the mental energy I used to normalize myself to do other things — including building connection.

That is the thing about Switzerland (or maybe, Western European communities in general): you cannot expect connections to build naturally with the people you interact daily. There has to be deliberate efforts in actually build these human connections. This is perhaps because social cliques in Europeans and Americans in general are much smaller. The rift between acquaintance and friend is huge.

I think it is somewhat fair. They value their time so much they refuse to spend time with people who they do not like or spending energy to seek approval to people they hardly knew. But again, this comes at a cost of alienation. My experience later showed that I was far from the only one who struggled with loneliness here in Geneva. Most people, after some deeper conversations, also seek for connections, but trapped with social norms that uphold individualism rather than collectivism.

So I decided to made those deliberate efforts. I had been aware that there were some Indonesians in the offices. I had heard faintly the names, so I reached coldly by email, saying hi. They replied and welcomed me, asking to meet for a coffee.

I also reached some of the coworker in my department. There are a lot of experts with cool stories to tell, once you asked them for a chat. My office floor is full of disease control departments, ranging from HIV/AIDS, Tuberculosis, and NTDs. Just small talks when heating lunch box in common pantry could unravel into stories like Ebola investigation in DR Congo to outbreak investigation in war-torn Ukraine.

With the WHO declared funding shortage, there has been official communication about restructurization. The efficiency measures spanned from changing upcoming extenral offline meeting to online and stricter recruitment posts. That includes intern — the programme officially came to a halt starting mid February 2025. Since then, no more incomings, only outgoings.

Interns pool in WHO HQ had been a fluid passage of incomings and outgoings. People come and go in rapid pace, some interns could have contract as short as 6 weeks, with others (including me) take the longest possible time (24 weeks). The relationship between us had been, in my observation, transient.

But the current crisis inside the Organization somehow bonded us. It looked like we had similar doubts about what to do with our careers after completing this internship. Previously, we had arranged at least once weekly lunch together just to stay in touch. But since March, it was almost every weekdays. We also hung out after hours, in pubs, jazz clubs, or even parks during the spring when the weather is nicer.

We came from completely differrent parts of the world, but still we worked here together for better world. That is multilateralism.*That*, is global health.

With the sunlight is getting longer, the snow melts, things actually started to be better. The loneliness started to diminish, interchanged with organic intercultural connections. At my own place, my landlord held various dinner parties, the classical western one with its specific rites in which I was not familiar before. I loved the other works, too. Things went so well.*Too well, *in fact.

### You only live once

It was April, and the internship would be over in six weeks. I have prepared for my next steps in life by applying for jobs. But no good news came to my inbox. I was so worried because I do not want to be unemployed for long, but the state of global health career market is dire for all levels. From this experience, I learned that working in global health overseas has been very challenging. The fact that I was there is not the rule. I was so lucky on securing this role just before the global health crisis unraveled.

The time is ticking and I was faced with two choices. One, to go all-in with job applications and spending the rest of spare time left in Geneva to apply for jobs. Two, I let go this job-seeking process and soak the remaining moments of me living in Geneva.

I chose the latter. Why? Because I only live once. To be able to live my mid-twenties in this city is a massive blessing, even if it is only for half a year. Second, the job market is so dire and my judgment calls were like “additional six weeks of intense job seeking, which could be done back home in Jakarta, would add very little chance of prolonging my stay in Geneva, anyway.” Lastly, even if by some massive luck I come back to Geneva to work in a senior role, it would*feel *different. I would have much bigger responsibilities then, less naivete about the world, and less energy to venture and fart around.

I stood by my decision. Those last weeks were one of the most joyful period I had in my life, so far. I woke up every day, feeling happy because I was lucky to be in this position, and do everything to every people to express how lucky I was to be there. The things I did with my friends were mundane: hiking, enjoying cheese and wine, swimming in the lake and cooking together. But, I loved it — every moment of it.

### This too shall pass

The time was finally up. I had to go back home. In the last weeks, I said farewell to every people I know in the city. It was so hard, but also such a good ending for my short stint here.

## Wrapping up

Geneva provided me with perhaps the most transformative personal journey to date. From being independent in a foreign country, struggling with loneliness, to enjoying solitude, building diverse networks, and thriving in doing the very thing I am passionate about. The contrast between the worst to best moments make this phase of life*the most psychologically rich experience*.

And with this, I wrapped up my personal journey of extended global health training, from learning in the field directly (Rote), in the classroom (London), and in meeting rooms formulating policies and recommendations (Geneva). It has been very much a humbling and life-changing journey, especially from a man in Jakarta who had never went out of his hometown before.

Below is my personal executive summary of the past two and a half years experience in three different locations. These locations have their own charms and icks. Each has its own value set to thrive, and each people will be suited with different locations (so I do not expect this review will be agreed with everyone).

![My own ratings for each phases](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/800/1*5IE5aQBd9nDdI78sgBxkow.jpeg)

*My own ratings for each phases*The simple reason why I chose Geneva as my personal favorite is because it is the very place that I could achieve my life goal — to live a dignified life. I think it speaks volumes when I, with an earning of less than half of cantonal minimum wage, could live more dignified life than me in Jakarta with three of even four times of it. For me, dignified simply means worthy of respect. It does not matter if you are a janitor, or a store clerk, or a director general of a UN agency — everyone deserve to be treated with respect, with respectable public services, public facilities, and shared orderliness that are taken care of by everyone. It is no wonder that Geneva is consistently placed top five among [the most livable cities in the world](https://www.economistgroup.com/press-centre/economist-intelligence/eiu-global-liveability-index-2025-copenhagen-replaces-vienna-as-worlds-most).

But again, this choice is personal. On the surface, Geneva is boring, the people are not that warm, and everything cost an arm and a leg here. To each their own.

### Music of Geneva

Cherry blossoms, quaint old towns, and picturesque lakes. I admit the songs in my Geneva playlist are not as diverse as my London or Rote playlists. That said, the emotional weight that the songs bring to me personally made this playlist as the most played in my phone by a long margin.

Emotional time travel, as I said before.

## Epilogue

Almost six months have gone by. After two and a half years wandering around, finally I have returned home for good. Different from previous returns, it is no longer a transit. I did a brief return trip to the UK for my graduation right after I went back from Geneva.

I felt both happy and sad. Happy that I could finally meet my family and friends back home, sad because I have to let go the life I had in the previous places.

Nobody prepared me to the fact that the worst culture shock that I have experienced was not when I stepped into Rote, nor London, not even Geneva. It is actually when I have returned to Jakarta. My hometown has given a humbling reality check. “Welcome to the real world,” they said.

But again, the past two and a half years were as real as it gets. It was an age of discovery. Age of venturing the depth of emotion, to self actualize. To discover one’s passion and chasing it. To understand different lived realities of different people throughout the globe, to*be*. And these years have taught me two valuable lessons.

First, it was always about the people. I am reminded of the [Harvard Study of Adult Development](https://www.adultdevelopmentstudy.org/), led by Dr Robert Waldinger that have been studying about life course and quality of life for more than 80 years. And from the good life in which he summarise the finding, all those people he studied in various courses of life, it has a consistent pattern of what is the reliable predictor of long-term happiness. It is not extravagant wealth, nor flood of validations from other people, it is the*quality of relationships with other people you can trust*.

And from these past years, I can finally attest to that myself. There is no way I could survive in Rote, one of the outermost island in Indonesia with very limited resource, if not from the kindness and strong bond in the community, be it from locals and fellow interns. I would probably not graduate my Master’s if my roomate did not encourage me to chase to the college campus within the hour after knowing I misread the exam schedule. And looking at how desperate and lonely I was in the first three months of my stay in Geneva, which seems paradoxical because materially, I live my best life objectively. Only after I got to know and build small communities with the people around, I could finally enjoy my life there.

This experience has taught me what to prioritize in life if my life goal is to live a dignified life. It is to invest in people around you. Be a person that you yourself want to spend time with (although admittedly I have been absolutely insufferable). Be fair to all people, in workplace and community. Be reliable. Be funny. Be an authentic self, unless the real you have sociopathic tendencies in which it is out of everyone’s best interest for you to seek professional help.

Second, find your crowd. It is quite a lonely venture to start an unconventional career path in global health as a physician. The pragmatic way in my professional circles has been to undergo a residency and becoming a specialist. After I came back here after years abroad — those judgment made total sense.

However, I still chose the path less traveled. Not only because I was being idealistic (I have to admit I still am). It is also because I*know *I belong here. I like the field, I can be good at it, and recent experience show that it aligns with my value better than becoming a clinician. Quoting from Mark Twain:

> “The two most important days in your life are the day you are born and the day you find out why.”

More than that, I definitely succeed in finding my crowd: people who I share my values and goals with. I do not have to justify my goals again. For the right people, the*right crowd*, you are just enough.
